Preface

This handbook grew out of a simple observation: most people who use AI tools in Bible Translation work are not getting nearly as much out of them as they could — not because the tools are inadequate, but because the instructions given to those tools are.

Prompting is the skill of giving AI precise, well-structured instructions that produce reliable, usable output. It is not a technical skill in the programmer sense. It is a communication skill — the same discipline that makes a good brief, a clear checking question, or a useful consultant report. If you can write a focused request to a colleague, you can learn to prompt well.

This handbook is the take-home companion to the Prompt Engineering session taught in the AILT 2026 AI Tools Track. It follows the same structure as the session: foundations first, then techniques, then application by role. Use it during the workshop. Keep it on your desk afterward. Adapt the templates for your team.

A note on scope: this handbook covers prompt engineering only. It does not cover Scripture Forge, AQuA, LM Studio, or other specific tools in the AI Tools track — those have their own guides. What it covers is the underlying skill that makes every one of those tools work better.

How to Use This Handbook

During the course: Each chapter maps to a segment of the live session. The appendices belong to the course too: use them as in-session aids, workshop handouts, and follow-up tools when you build your own prompts in the labs.

After the session: The appendices are designed as standalone quick-reference cards. Appendix D (Prompt Template Library) in particular is meant to be photocopied, laminated, or pinned to a wall — a working tool that extends the course into daily practice.

For your team: Chapter 5 (Prompting by Lane) and Appendix C (Lane Reference Card) are the most shareable sections. They require no prior reading to be useful.

The Three Lanes

Throughout this handbook, examples and techniques are tagged by role:

Tag Lane Who
Lane 1 Translation & Language Mother Tongue Translators · Translators · Linguists · Literacy Workers
Lane 2 Consultants & Advisors Translation Consultants · Consultants in Training · Exegetes · Advisors
Lane 3 Leadership & Technical Cluster Managers · Project Executives · IT Support · Software QA · Facilitators

Not every technique section will have an example for every lane. Where a technique is especially important for a specific lane, it is called out. All techniques apply to all lanes — the examples just illustrate the most common application.

The Through-Line: Mark 5:1–20

To make the principles concrete and comparable across examples, this handbook uses a single shared passage: Mark 5:1–20, the account of Jesus and the man possessed by "Legion" among the tombs. This passage was selected because it:

  • Is culturally rich and translation-challenging (pigs, tombs, Legion, Decapolis)
  • Contains a named and unusual key term ("Legion") that requires careful rendering
  • Involves a cultural context (honour/shame, purity laws, Roman military presence) that varies significantly across target audiences
  • Is familiar enough to most participants that the focus stays on the prompting technique, not on parsing the content

Where your own work uses different passages or different challenges, substitute freely. The structures transfer directly.

A Note on How This Handbook Was Made

This handbook was written with the assistance of AI tools — which is fitting, given its subject matter. Content was developed through a guided dialogue between the author and an AI assistant: the author supplied the domain knowledge, the structure, the examples from field practice, and the theological and methodological judgments; the AI assisted with drafting, language refinement, and working through the implications of each section. Every section was reviewed, corrected, and approved by the author before being included.

Specifically, AI was used for: - Guided content generation — developing section drafts from author-supplied outlines, notes, and direction - Language improvement — improving clarity and fluency in a second-language writing context - Fact-checking support — though all claims about tools, field practice, and Scripture were verified by the author independently

This is itself an example of the Informed Pilot posture described in Chapter 1: the author remained accountable for every judgment in this text. The AI was a capable assistant, not an authority.

Where any error survives into this text, it is the author's responsibility to correct it.

A Word About AI and Scripture

AI is a powerful assistant. It is not a new authority over Scripture.

When an AI output disagrees with the text, your exegetical judgment — supported by Paratext, Logos, your consultants, and your language community — is the tie-breaker. AI accelerates the process. The standard of truth rests with you.

This handbook will return to this point at each stage where the risk of over-reliance is highest.
