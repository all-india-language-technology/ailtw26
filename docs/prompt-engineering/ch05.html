Lane 1 · Translation & Language Practitioners

Mother Tongue Translators · Translators · Linguists · Literacy Workers

Scenario A — Cultural & Historical Background (FIA Support)

Task: Before drafting Mark 5:1–20, you need to understand the cultural and historical background of the pigs in the story — specifically for an audience where pigs represent significant household wealth.

Weak prompt
Tell me about pigs in the Bible.
🛠️

What would you improve?

Discuss before seeing the strong version: what is this prompt missing?

Strong prompt
Act as a biblical scholar specialised in Second Temple Judaism and Mediterranean economics. I am preparing a translation team for drafting Mark 5:1–20. Our target community is a group where pigs represent significant household wealth — comparable to cattle in other agricultural societies. Explain: (1) What the pig herd would have meant to the local economy in first-century Decapolis (2) What cultural weight the destruction of 2,000 pigs carries for a landowner (3) Why that detail matters for how the passage will be received by a community that prizes pigs Use plain English. Do not speculate beyond what the text and historical record support. Do not cite specific reference codes — I will verify those independently. Return as 3 numbered paragraphs, one per focus point above. Maximum 80 words each.
P Persona CX Context G Guardrail F Format
✅
What changed: Persona grounds the language. Three focus points create structure before output is generated. Cultural anchor pulls the response toward the actual translation challenge. Guardrails prevent fabricated citations. Format prevents a sprawling essay.

Scenario B — Storyboard Scripting for Oral / Literacy Contexts

Task: You want to brief a local illustrator on a 6-panel stick-figure series for Mark 5:1–20 for oral community engagement. AI can't draw — but it can write the brief.

Common mistake
Draw me a storyboard for Mark 5:1–20.
Strong prompt
For Mark 5:1–20, create a detailed storyboard script for a local artist to use as a brief for a 6-panel stick-figure illustration series. For each panel provide: (1) The setting (2) The key characters present (3) The main action (4) One short caption in plain language (maximum 10 words) Capture the key narrative movements in sequence: arrival by boat, encounter at the tombs, dialogue with Legion, the pigs, the healed man, and the sending. Do not add theological interpretation to the captions — describe only what can be seen.
CX Context F Format CT Chain of Thought G Guardrail
✅
What changed: The task is correctly scoped as scripting, not drawing. The 6-panel structure and 4-part per-panel format are defined before the model begins. The guardrail keeps captions visual rather than interpretive.
🌐
Low-resource language note for Lane 1: When asking for illustrative examples or idioms in your target language, treat AI output as a first draft only. Ask a fluent mother-tongue speaker to verify before using. AI knowledge of your language community's oral patterns may be thin or absent.

Lane 2 · Consultants & Advisors

Translation Consultants · Consultants in Training · Exegetes · Advisors

Scenario A — Key-Term Analysis with Chain of Thought

Task: Identifying key terms in Mark 5:1–20 that carry significant translation risk — with traceable reasoning you can audit.

Weak prompt
What are the key theological terms in Mark 5 that might be hard to translate?
🛠️

What's missing?

How would the AI define "hard to translate" on its own? What four-step reasoning would produce a usable output?

Strong prompt
Act as a translation consultant with expertise in New Testament Greek semantics. I am reviewing key terms in Mark 5:1–20 for a translation team working in a shame-honour culture in South Asia. We are at checking stage 2. From the ESV text below, work through the following steps: Step 1 — Identify all theologically significant terms: words whose precise rendering will significantly affect doctrinal understanding. Step 2 — For each term: describe its semantic range in plain language. Do not fabricate Greek dictionary codes. Step 3 — For each term: identify the translation risk if rendered too narrowly vs too broadly. Step 4 — Suggest one checking question the translation team could use to verify the rendering. [paste ESV text of Mark 5:1–20] Do not speculate. Flag uncertain items as NEEDS VERIFICATION. Return as: Term | Semantic Range | Narrowing Risk | Broadening Risk | Checking Question.
P Persona CX Context CT Chain of Thought G Guardrail F Format
✅
What changed: The four-step CoT forces the model through all analytical layers before producing any output. The checking-question column transforms the output into a directly usable tool — not just a report to read but a rubric to apply.

Scenario B — Back-Translation Review for Implicit Information

Task: Reviewing a back-translation to identify where meaning present in the source has been weakened or lost.

Weak prompt
Compare this back-translation with the source text and tell me if there are any issues.
Strong prompt
Act as a senior translation consultant. Below are three texts for Mark 5:1–20: the ESV source text, the current draft, and the back-translation. [paste all three, clearly labelled] Step 1 — Compare the back-translation to the ESV source. Identify any place where information present in the ESV is absent from or weakened in the back-translation. Step 2 — For each gap: assess whether it is (a) an implicit-information issue in the draft, (b) a back-translation accuracy issue, or (c) both. Step 3 — For each gap: suggest a specific checking question the consultant could use with the translation team to surface the issue. Do not flag minor stylistic variations. Focus only on meaning gaps. Do not speculate about intent. Return as: Verse | Gap Description | Type (a/b/both) | Checking Question.
P Persona CT Chain of Thought G Guardrail F Format

Lane 3 · Technical & Leadership

Cluster Managers · Senior Executives · IT & Language Tech · Project Facilitators · QA

Scenario A — Meeting Notes → Action List

Task: Extract structured action items from five emails from this week.

Weak prompt
Look through my emails and prepare a to-do list for me.
❌
The AI has no access to your inbox. This produces a confused refusal or a generic template — because the materials (the emails themselves) are not in the conversation.
Strong prompt
I am pasting 5 emails from this week below. For each email, identify: (1) Any action required from me (2) The deadline if mentioned (3) Who else is involved Ignore greetings, signatures, and social pleasantries — extract only actionable content. Return as a markdown checklist table: Action | Owner | Deadline | Waiting On. [paste all 5 emails, each clearly labelled with its sender and date]
CX Context CT Chain of Thought G Guardrail F Format
✅
What changed: The data is supplied. The extraction categories are defined. The guardrail prevents over-processing social content. The table format produces a deliverable that can be pasted into any project management tool immediately.

Scenario B — AI Tool Rollout Readiness Checklist

Task: You are preparing to introduce a new AI tool to a field translation team and need a readiness checklist.

Weak prompt
Give me a checklist for rolling out an AI tool.
Strong prompt
Act as a deployment project manager in a field technology context. I am preparing to introduce LM Studio (local AI tool) to a translation team of 12 people in a low-bandwidth environment. Hardware: i5 laptops, 16GB RAM, no dedicated GPU. Internet: intermittent 3G. Team profile: experienced with Paratext, new to AI tools. Generate a rollout readiness checklist covering: (1) Pre-deployment infrastructure checks (2) Team training requirements (3) First-session facilitation plan (4) Risk flags specific to low-bandwidth deployment (5) Success criteria for a 30-day pilot Do not recommend steps that require dedicated GPU or stable broadband — all suggestions must be feasible within the hardware constraints above. Return as a numbered checklist grouped by the 5 sections above.

C3 · Challenge

Cross-lane exercises

These three exercises are designed for cross-lane pairs. Find someone from a different lane.

👥 Pair with someone in a different lane for all three exercises

Exercise 1 — Prompt Swap

Each person shows their strongest prompt from today's sessions to their cross-lane partner.

🔄

Discussion questions

  • Can your partner understand what the prompt is trying to accomplish — without explanation?
  • What is unclear? What would your partner change?
  • What does your lane need from a prompt that their lane doesn't? What do you have in common?

Exercise 2 — Translate the Output

Take a Lane 2 checking output — a formatted table of translation issues from the key-term analysis scenario above. Now ask Lane 3: "How would you present this to a project executive who is not a translation specialist?"

🔄

Build a new prompt together

Lane 2 person: explain what needs to be preserved. Lane 3 person: explain what the executive audience needs instead. Together: write a prompt that transforms the checking table into an executive summary.

Try it. What did the output look like? What would you change?

Exercise 3 — Build a Shared AI Session Brief

What would an ai-session-brief.md look like that is genuinely useful to both a translator and a project manager working on the same project?

🛠️

Draft one together — 10 minutes

  • What sections does the translator need? What does the project manager need?
  • Where do the guardrails overlap? Where are they different?
  • What would a section called "Non-negotiables" include that applies to every role on the team?

C5 · Closure
"Translators, consultants, and tech leads all touch the same draft. AI is most powerful when it connects what role boundaries sometimes divide."
The best prompt any of us can write is incomplete without the other roles. That is the real lesson of this chapter.

Lane 1 leaves with

FIA acceleration, better first-draft quality, skills to assess and improve any draft using AI as a structured research assistant — not an oracle.

Lane 2 leaves with

Faster issue discovery, repeatable review rubrics, Chain of Thought prompts that produce auditable reasoning — not verdicts without evidence.

Lane 3 leaves with

Tool selection confidence, governance clarity, a data safety decision rule, and a team-level deployment model that doesn't depend on individual best-practice.
