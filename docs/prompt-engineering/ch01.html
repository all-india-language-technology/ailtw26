A translator tried this.
Read the response carefully.

She pasted a reference question into ChatGPT and got an answer that looked ready to use in checking notes. Read the prompt first, then the response. Notice what makes it feel trustworthy.

The prompt typed
What does Louw-Nida say about the Greek word akathartos in Mark 5:2? What semantic domain code should I use in my checking notes?
The AI response
According to Louw and Nida's Greek-English Lexicon (1988), the word akathartos (ἀκάθαρτος) in Mark 5:2 falls under semantic domain 88 (Moral and Ethical Qualities and Related Behaviour), subdomain 88.24, defined as "pertaining to being ritually or morally unclean or impure." The code is 88.24. In the context of Mark 5, this domain captures both the ritual impurity of the demonised man (unclean by contact with tombs) and the spiritual impurity of the unclean spirit itself. For your checking notes, domain 88.24 accurately anchors the theological weight of the term.
🔎
Pause before you trust it. This answer sounds specific, scholarly, and confident. That is exactly why this example matters. AI can produce responses that look authoritative even when some details may be inaccurate, invented, or based on assumptions you did not notice. Before you rely on an answer like this, ask which claims would need to be verified.
💬

Discuss with your group — before we go further

  1. Does this response look trustworthy to you? What signals make it feel authoritative?
  2. What would you do with this information if you were writing checking notes?
  3. Is there anything in the response that makes you pause?
⚠️
The problem: The domain code 88.24 in this response may be entirely fabricated. The model generated a plausible-looking scholarly reference — with confidence, precision, and contextual explanation — that could be partially or wholly invented. The danger is not only that the answer may be wrong; it is that it feels ready to trust before it has been checked against the real source.
C2 · Content

What the machine is actually doing

Understanding the mechanism behind LLMs is not theory — it is the reason every prompting technique in this module works. Three core concepts, one analogy, and a radar of six failure modes.

📜

The Scribe Analogy

Imagine a student who copied ten thousand Bible commentaries, lexicons, and theological journals by hand — but never learned to read. From sheer pattern repetition, they know exactly which words tend to follow which other words in scholarly theological writing.

Ask them "The Lord is my…" and they write "shepherd" instantly — not because they understand Psalm 23, but because that word pair appeared millions of times.

Ask them something rare — a question about a minority language, a newly discovered manuscript, a community practice never written down — and they will produce a fluent, confident answer that may be entirely fabricated. They are not lying. They complete patterns. Truth and plausibility feel identical to them.

Three concepts that change how you prompt

🔢

Tokens, not words

The model counts tokens — roughly one common word each, but punctuation marks and rare words each split into fragments. A comma is a token. "Incomprehensible" is four tokens. This affects cost, context limits, and speed.

🪟

Context window

Everything the AI can see at once: your instructions, the conversation history, any text you've pasted. Modern windows hold hundreds of pages — but older content gets weaker attention as sessions grow.

📍

Vocabulary = location

Words the model has seen together live near each other in its internal space. Use "consultant's analysis" and it navigates toward structured, precise output. Use "explain" and it navigates toward general prose.

Nine factors shape every AI output. Four are yours.

Most of these are fixed before you open the tool. Focus your energy where you have real leverage.

Fixed by your provider — not in your hands

Training data

What the model learned from — determines knowledge, defaults, bias

Fine-tuning purpose

What the model was further trained for after initial training

Provider configuration

Platform-level filters, limits, and features your provider set

Knowledge cutoff

The date beyond which the model has no training data

Available tools (partly)

Web search, file reading — partly set by provider; some are your choice

Risk Radar: Six failure modes every BT practitioner must know

These are not hypothetical. Each has caused real problems in Bible Translation work.

💥 Failure Mode 1: Hallucination ›

What it is: The model generates authoritative-sounding content that is factually invented — fake Louw-Nida domain codes, non-existent scholarly citations, fabricated Greek glosses.

Why it happens: The model optimises for plausibility, not truth. When it cannot reliably answer, it completes the pattern of what a confident answer looks like.

Why it is dangerous in BT: A hallucinated code looks exactly like a real one. It can enter checking documentation and survive into publication.

Triggers it
What does Louw-Nida say about akathartos in Mark 5:2?
Safer alternative
Explain the semantic range of the Greek word akathartos as used in Mark 5. Do not cite specific reference codes — I will verify those independently. If you are uncertain about any claim, say so explicitly.
📅 Failure Mode 2: Knowledge Cutoff ›

What it is: The model's training ends at a fixed date — typically 12–18 months before you are using it. Events, publications, and tool updates after that date simply do not exist for it.

Why it matters in BT: New Paratext versions, updated UBS guidelines, recently completed surveys, your team's latest key-term decisions — the model knows nothing of these.

Triggers it
What are the latest Paratext 9.5 features I should use for my checking workflow?
Safer alternative
I am pasting the Paratext 9.5 release notes below. Based only on this content, summarise the checking features most relevant to a translation team working on a minority language project. [paste notes]
🧭 Failure Mode 3: Bias & Worldview ›

What it is: The model was trained predominantly on Western, English-language internet text. Its default theological frame, cultural illustrations, and metaphors lean Western Protestant. It does not know this about itself.

Why it matters in BT: When you ask for "a culturally appropriate illustration," the model's default is the culture embedded in its training — which may be entirely foreign to your target audience.

The rule: Always anchor the cultural frame explicitly. Name the people group, the social dynamics, what is not familiar in your context.

🌐
Low-resource language reality: AI performance degrades significantly for languages with small digital footprints. If your target language has little or no presence in training data, hallucination risk increases and grammatical suggestions become unreliable. Always have a mother-tongue speaker review any AI output in the target language.
🔢 Failure Mode 4: Math & Counting ›

What it is: LLMs process numbers as tokens, not values. They are statistically unreliable for arithmetic, syllable counts, verse tallying, word frequency analysis.

Why it matters in BT: A wrong verse count or misreported key-term frequency can introduce errors into checking documentation.

The rule: Delegate counting and arithmetic to reliable tools (Paratext's Find, a spreadsheet). Bring the AI back in to help you interpret the pattern once you have the verified count.

🔍 Failure Mode 5: Attention Fade ›

What it is: The "attention" mechanism underlying all modern LLMs gives progressively less weight to older text in a conversation. Tokens that are far apart receive weaker mutual attention. Your instructions from the start of a session gradually lose influence — even while still technically in the window.

How it shows up: Gradual loosening of constraints. Speculation increases incrementally. Flags appear less frequently. Harder to spot than Context Loss because there's no clear before-and-after moment.

The rule: Repeat key constraints at the start of a new task within a session. See Appendix F for the attention mechanism explained in plain English.

🌊 Failure Mode 6: Context Loss ›

What it is: As a session grows longer, the oldest content is silently dropped from the context window to make room for new content. The model does not warn you. It has no awareness of what is gone.

The silent failure problem: The model cannot detect an absence. It continues with full confidence, operating under fewer constraints than you set, with no indication anything has changed. The onus falls entirely on you to notice.

Why it matters in BT: The guardrails you set at the start — "do not speculate beyond the text," "flag uncertain interpretations" — are the first things to disappear as sessions grow.

The rule: Long session? Refresh your constraints. Or better still: start a new chat per task. See Chapter 4 for the full defence system.

C3 · Challenge

Spot the failure mode

Three AI responses. Each contains a failure mode. Can you identify which one — before you see the analysis?

Response A

The community testing of this draft was completed last month. Based on the June 2025 UBS guidelines, the checking team should use the new simplified back-translation protocol introduced in the latest Paratext workshop guidelines.
🤔

What failure mode is this? Discuss with your group.

What is the AI claiming to know? What problem does that create for a translation team that trusts this response?

📅
Failure Mode 2: Knowledge Cutoff. The AI refers to "June 2025 UBS guidelines" and recent workshop events as if they are current. Depending on the model's training cutoff, these may not exist yet — or the model may be describing an older version of guidelines as if they were recent. The response names a timeframe to create credibility, but the model has no knowledge of events after its cutoff.

Response B

For a community where pigs represent wealth, the most effective illustration of the grace shown in Mark 5:19 would be the father forgiving a child's debt — similar to the Prodigal Son narrative. This speaks powerfully to the individual conscience and personal relationship with God that lies at the heart of this community's experience.
🤔

What failure mode is this? Discuss with your group.

The response answers the question, but something is wrong with the frame it uses. What assumption has it made about the community?

🧭
Failure Mode 3: Bias & Worldview. "Individual conscience and personal relationship with God" is a Western Protestant framing. A community organised around honour, shame, and kinship obligations — common in many contexts where BT teams work — experiences grace and debt in entirely different social frameworks. The model defaulted to its training data's cultural center without flagging the assumption.

Response C

The word "spirit" (πνεῦμα, pneuma) appears 7 times in the Gospel of Mark. In Mark 5:1–20 specifically, it appears 4 times, referring to the unclean spirit possessing the man. The frequency of this term is significant for establishing a key-term rendering, as it is the most repeated theological descriptor in this pericope.
🤔

What failure mode is this? Discuss with your group.

The numbers sound specific and authoritative. What is the problem with trusting them?

🔢
Failure Mode 4: Math & Counting. The model is not actually counting. It is predicting what a plausible word-count response looks like. The numbers (7 times in Mark, 4 times in Mark 5) are presented with full confidence but may be wrong. Never trust AI word counts. Use Paratext's Find function to get the actual number, then bring the AI back to help you interpret the pattern.
💡
Key takeaway from this exercise: All three responses sounded credible. None of them warned you that something was wrong. The responsibility for detecting failure falls entirely on you — the AI cannot audit its own outputs.
