C2 · Content
Your Reference Card + Two New Skills
⏱ 00:05 – 00:15 · 10 minutes
📌 Six Building Blocks — the anatomy of a strong prompt
P
Persona — Who is the AI acting as?
Sets the vocabulary, expertise, and depth of the response. Be specific — not just "an expert."
"You are a biblical scholar specialising in 1st-century Jewish culture…"
CX
Context — What is your situation?
Who you are, your project, your target audience. Overrides the model's generic assumptions.
"We are translating the Gospel of Mark into a Tibeto-Burman language. We use GNTD as our primary source…"
→
Task — the core of every prompt
The specific thing you are asking. Be precise about scope, subject, and deliverable.
"List the 5 most important exegetical issues to check in this passage, with 2–3 checking questions each…"
G
Guardrails — What should the AI NOT do?
Prevents speculation, hallucination, jargon, and theological overreach. Essential for BT work.
"Do not speculate beyond the text. Flag anything uncertain as NEEDS VERIFICATION."
F
Format — How should the response be structured?
Table, checklist, sections, word limit. Saves reformatting and makes output directly usable.
"Write in two labelled sections: (1) Cultural background and (2) Footnote draft — under 60 words."
FS
Few-Shot — Show one perfect example
One well-chosen example teaches style and format more precisely than a paragraph of description.
"Here is a good checking question: '…' — write one for the next verse in exactly this pattern."
CT
Chain of Thought — Make it reason step-by-step
Forces the model to show its reasoning — so you can catch an error before it corrupts the conclusion.
"Work through this step by step. Step 1 — … Step 2 — … Only then, make your recommendation."
Not every prompt needs all six. A simple task may need only a Persona and a Guardrail. In this session we focus on: Persona · Context · Guardrails · Format
📎
New Skill 1: Upload a Document
Upload a commentary excerpt, lexicon entry, or project notes. Then ask:

"Based only on this document, explain…"

The AI reasons from your source — not its general training. You control the frame of reference.
🔗
New Skill 2: Paste a Link
Paste a URL as your reference source:

"Using this article, summarise the background to [passage]…"

⚠️ Not all tools read links the same way. Some fetch the page; some don't. Test before you rely on it.
📎 vs 🔗  Upload — private files, PDFs, project documents.  |  Link — public web articles, online lexicons, open commentaries. Note which your tool handles reliably.

Prompt — built using the 6 building blocks
Persona You are an experienced exegetical consultant who has supported Bible translation teams working in Asian minority languages.
Context We are a 3-member translation team working in a NE tribal (Tibeto-Burman) language. We produced an NT draft using a Scripture Forge-generated text, with GNTD as our primary source. We also consult NIV, TNN, and other versions. Our team has Bible college training and 6–8 years of translation experience, but we have not had formal training in meaning-based translation principles.
Task Help us design a systematic process for checking our translation draft exegetically. Give us the 5–6 most important categories of exegetical issues to examine in each passage, and for each category, provide 2–3 specific checking questions our team can work through together.
Guardrails Use plain, practical language — no academic technical terms our team would not know. If any category is especially important given our use of GNTD as the source text, flag it clearly.
Format Present the output as a reusable checking guide — each category as a clear heading, with checking questions listed beneath it.

👫 Round 2 — Your Turn, In Pairs  ·  00:25–00:35
Scenario · submitted by a participant In Paratext's RSV+ Enhanced resources, only the word dictionary is linked — it won't explain the cultural weight behind phrases and idioms. In Mark 5:9, the demon names itself "Legion, for we are many." We know Legion is a Roman military term, but what did that word carry for a Jewish audience living under occupation? And how do we write a footnote that brings that weight across into our language?
👫 In pairs — 6 minutes
Build a prompt that gives you what you need for a real passage you're working on.
Use a document upload or a link. Use the building blocks from the reference card — you don't need all six.
▶  Facilitator — click to reveal example prompt after teams share
Example prompt — Mark 5:9 · "Legion" · 6 building blocks
Persona You are a scholar of 1st-century Jewish culture and New Testament background, with expertise in how Roman political realities shaped the language and imagery of the Gospels.
Context We are translating the Gospel of Mark into [our language]. In Mark 5:9, the unclean spirit identifies itself as "Legion, for we are many." "Legion" is a Latin loanword for a Roman military unit of approximately 6,000 soldiers. Our readers may be unfamiliar with Roman occupation. We need to understand what this word meant to a 1st-century Jewish audience in order to make a good translation decision and to write a useful footnote.
Task Explain the cultural and political significance of "Legion" in Mark 5:9: (1) what did this word mean to a Jewish person living under Roman occupation, and why does the demon's self-identification as "Legion" carry more weight than simply meaning "many"? (2) What are the main translation options — transliterate, use a native equivalent, or explain in the text — and what are the trade-offs of each?
Guardrails Avoid academic jargon. Base your answer on widely accepted biblical scholarship. If scholars interpret this term differently, note that briefly.
Format Write in two clearly labelled sections: (1) Cultural background — for the translator's reference, and (2) Footnote draft — under 60 words, in plain language suitable for general Bible readers.
