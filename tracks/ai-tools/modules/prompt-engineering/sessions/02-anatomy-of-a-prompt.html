C2 · Content

Six building blocks

Specificity is the single most important principle in prompting. Every technique below is a different way of being specific about what you need.

Building block colours used in prompt examples: P Persona CX Context G Guardrail F Format FS Few-Shot CT Chain of Thought
P Building Block 1 · Persona (Role Assignment) ›

What it does: Tells the model who to be for this task — setting the vocabulary, expertise level, default framing, and depth of response. Without a role, the model defaults to a generic helpful assistant aiming at a general audience.

How to use it: Start with "Act as a…" or "You are a…" followed by a specific, relevant expert description.

Common mistake: "Act as an expert" is too vague. "Act as a biblical scholar specialised in Second Temple Judaism writing for a translation team in a shame-honour culture" gives the model substantial, useful framing.

Weak (no persona)
Identify naturalness issues in this draft of Mark 5:3–5.
Strong (with persona)
Act as a mother-tongue speaker of [language name] hearing this passage for the first time as spoken narrative — not as written text. Identify up to 3 phrases that would sound unnatural to an oral listener.

By lane:

Lane 1

"Act as a rural mother-tongue translator encountering this passage for the first time…"

Lane 2

"Act as a senior translation consultant specialised in Biblical Greek exegesis…"

Lane 3

"Act as a technical project manager writing a status update for a non-technical stakeholder…"

F Building Block 2 · Output Format ›

What it does: Tells the model how to structure the response — table, checklist, numbered list, prose, JSON. Left unspecified, the model defaults to flowing prose, which is often harder to use directly.

Why it matters in BT work: You frequently need output that can be pasted directly into Paratext notes, an Excel sheet, or a checking report. Specifying format saves manual reformatting and makes output immediately actionable.

Format instructionWhen to use
"Return as a 3-column table: Verse | Issue | Suggestion"Checking notes, issue logs
"Provide a bulleted checklist with a checkbox for each action item"Meeting follow-up, task lists
"Number each point. No prose introduction or conclusion."Quick analysis, structured review
"Return one paragraph, 100 words maximum"Executive summary, quick brief
"Format as valid USFM. Output only the USFM — no explanation."Paratext import, scripture entry
G Building Block 3 · Guardrails (Negative Constraints) ›

What it does: Tells the model what not to do. Guardrails are the safety fence that prevents speculation, hallucination, theological overreach, and inappropriate register. In Bible Translation, guardrails are not optional.

Core guardrails for BT work:

  • T
    Theological constraint "Do not speculate beyond the text. If you are uncertain about any interpretation, flag it as NEEDS VERIFICATION."
  • R
    Reference constraint "Do not cite specific Louw-Nida codes, Greek dictionary entries, or scholar names. I will verify those independently."
  • Rg
    Register constraint "Do not use theological jargon (e.g. 'eschatological', 'expiation'). Use plain English accessible to a primary-school reader."
    • 🌡️
    Temperature in plain language: "Be precise and literal. Do not speculate or be creative. Give one definitive answer." — use this for all exegesis and checking work. Never use a "creative" instruction for accuracy-sensitive tasks.
    FS Building Block 4 · Few-Shot Prompting (Teaching by Example) ›

    What it does: Instead of describing the style you want in abstract terms, you show the model one perfect example and ask it to follow that exact pattern. One well-chosen example communicates your team's style, vocabulary, and format more precisely than a paragraph of description.

    When to use it: When your team has an established style that differs from a generic template, or when zero-shot outputs are consistently missing something you cannot easily describe.

    Zero-shot (no example)
    Write a checking question for Mark 5:3.
    One-shot (with example)
    Here is a good checking question for Mark 5:2: "Where did the possessed man come from when he met Jesus?" Now write a checking question for Mark 5:3 following this exact pattern: one sentence, plain language, observable fact, no interpretation.
    CT Building Block 5 · Chain of Thought ›

    What it does: Instructs the model to map out its reasoning step-by-step before arriving at a final answer. This prevents premature conclusions, exposes assumptions at each stage, and gives you traceable reasoning you can audit and correct.

    Why it matters in BT: If the model skips straight to a recommendation, you cannot tell whether it understood all the relevant layers. If it reasons out loud, step by step, you can catch an error at Step 1 before it corrupts the final recommendation.

    Without Chain of Thought
    What are some plausible ways of expressing "Legion" in Mark 5:9 in my translation?
    With Chain of Thought
    Work through this step by step before making a recommendation: Step 1 — What did "Legion" mean to a first-century Roman audience? (military scale, organised power, dread) Step 2 — What does the use of this specific word communicate about the nature and scale of this man's possession? Step 3 — What concept in [target language/culture] communicates "an overwhelming, innumerable, terrifying spiritual force"? Only after completing all three steps, suggest 2–3 translation options with your reasoning for each.
    🔍
    Lane 2 note: Chain of Thought is the most important technique for consultants and exegetes. It transforms the AI from an oracle delivering verdicts into a traceable research assistant whose reasoning can be audited, challenged, and corrected.
    CX Building Block 6 · Context (Situational Background) ›

    What it does: Tells the model the situation you are in — who you are, what project this is for, what constraints apply, who will use the output. Situational context calibrates how the model reasons before it begins.

    A simple test: If you handed your prompt to a stranger with no other information, would they have enough to do the task well? If not, the missing information is your context.

    Without context
    Act as a translation consultant. Review this back-translation of Mark 5:1–5 and identify naturalness issues.
    With context
    Act as a translation consultant. I am working in Tamil with a mother-tongue team in rural Tamil Nadu. We are at checking stage 2, preparing for a consultant check next week. Our community has a strong oral storytelling tradition — the translation should sound like a story told aloud, not a formal written document. Review this back-translation of Mark 5:1–5 and identify any phrases that would sound stilted when read aloud.

    Which building blocks for which task?

    Task typeRecommended building blocks
    Exegesis / key-term analysisP CX G CT
    Checking question generationP CX F FS
    Cultural background researchP CX G F
    Back-translation reviewP CX G F CT
    Administrative summaryCX F FS
    🌐
    Minority language moment: Building blocks work for any language — but their effectiveness depends on how much training data exists. For low-resource languages, the Persona and Context blocks matter even more, because you must explicitly compensate for the model's thin knowledge of your language community. Never assume the AI knows your target cultural context. Say it.
    C3 · Challenge

    Repair the prompt

    Three weak prompts. Before you see the strong version: discuss with your group what is missing, and what you would add.

    Repair Prompt 1 — A Translator's Task

    Task: You want feedback on whether your draft of Mark 5:3–5 sounds natural for an oral audience.

    Weak prompt
    Check if this translation is natural. [draft pasted]
    🛠️

    Before seeing the answer

    What is this prompt missing? Which of the six building blocks would improve it? Sketch a better version together.

    Strong prompt
    Act as a mother-tongue speaker of [language name] hearing this passage for the first time as spoken narrative — not as written text. Below is a draft rendering of Mark 5:3–5. [paste draft] Our target audience are first-generation literate adults in rural [region]. The translation should sound like a story told aloud, not a formal written document. Identify up to 3 phrases that would sound unnatural, awkward, or overly formal to an oral listener. For each phrase: quote it exactly, explain what sounds wrong, and suggest a more natural alternative. Do not change the meaning of any phrase — only adjust for naturalness. Flag any suggestion you are uncertain about. Return as: Phrase | Problem | Suggested Alternative.
    P Persona CX Context G Guardrail F Format
    ✅
    What changed: The persona is an oral listener, not a written-language proofreader. The context anchors the target community. The guardrail prevents the AI from "improving" the meaning beyond what you asked. The format creates a directly usable table.

    Repair Prompt 2 — A Consultant's Task

    Task: You want to identify key terms in Mark 5:1–20 that carry significant translation risk.

    Weak prompt
    What are the key terms in Mark 5 that might be hard to translate?
    🛠️

    Before seeing the answer

    How would the model define "hard to translate" on its own? What's missing? Which building blocks should be added?

    Strong prompt
    Act as a translation consultant with expertise in New Testament Greek semantics. I am conducting a key-term review of Mark 5:1–20 for a translation team working in a shame-honour culture in South Asia. We are at checking stage 2. From the passage below, work through the following steps: Step 1 — Identify all theologically significant terms: defined as words whose precise rendering will significantly affect doctrinal understanding. Step 2 — For each term: describe its semantic range in plain language. Do not fabricate Greek dictionary codes. Step 3 — For each term: identify the translation risk if rendered too narrowly vs too broadly. Step 4 — Suggest one checking question for each term that a translation team could use to verify the rendering. [paste ESV text of Mark 5:1–20] Do not speculate. Flag uncertain items as NEEDS VERIFICATION. Return as: Term | Semantic Range | Narrowing Risk | Broadening Risk | Checking Question.
    P Persona CX Context CT Chain of Thought G Guardrail F Format
    ✅
    What changed: Chain of Thought forces the model through four analytical layers. The checking-question column transforms the output into a directly usable tool — not just a report. Term + Risks + Checking Question = something you can act on immediately.
    👥 Cross-lane pair activity — find someone in a different lane

    Repair Prompt 3 — A Leadership Task

    Task: Convert this week's cluster meeting notes into a formal governance report for a senior executive.

    Weak prompt
    Summarise this meeting. [notes pasted]
    🛠️

    Build this one together across lanes

    With your cross-lane partner: the Lane 3 person explains what a governance report looks like. The Lane 1 or 2 person helps design the guardrails and format. Build the strong prompt together.

    Strong prompt
    Act as a project manager writing a formal governance report for a non-technical executive audience. Here is an example of the report format we use for this project: "— Ben to send revised project timeline to all leads by Friday. Owner: Ben. Deadline: Friday 13 Mar." Using exactly this format (dash + action + Owner + Deadline), extract and list the action items from the following meeting notes. [paste raw notes] Do not insert information not present in the notes. Do not use technical jargon. Return in the same section structure as the example above: Action | Owner | Deadline | Status.
    P Persona FS Few-Shot G Guardrail F Format
    ✅
    What changed: Few-Shot learning replaces the need to describe the report format in abstract terms. One previous report entry communicates structure, vocabulary, and register more precisely than a paragraph of description.

    C5 · Closure
    "Specificity is not pedantry. It is care for the work."
    Every vague prompt hands your defaults to a machine. Every specific prompt asserts your judgment.
