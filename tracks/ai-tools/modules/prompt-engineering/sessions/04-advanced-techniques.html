The session that nobody noticed was failing.

A checking team used AI to support a three-day consultant review of Mark 5. Read what happened — turn by turn.

  • 1
    Turn 1 — Session start The consultant pasted the AI session brief, loaded key-term decisions, and began with a strong instruction: "Do not speculate beyond the text. Flag all uncertain interpretations as NEEDS VERIFICATION. Do not suggest renderings for terms not in our key-terms file." The AI followed every rule in the first ten exchanges.
  • 25
    Turn 25 — Mid-session The team had worked through Mark 5:1–12. The AI was still largely on-point, but started occasionally offering rendering suggestions for terms not in the key-terms file. The team assumed it was "just being helpful." Nobody re-read the original constraints.
  • 48
    Turn 48 — Late session The team reached Mark 5:19 — "Go home to your people and tell them how much the Lord has done for you." The AI produced a full theological commentary on the passage, including speculation about whether the man's witness constitutes an early example of Gentile mission. No flags. No NEEDS VERIFICATION. The original constraints had silently faded.
  • 72
    Three days later The checking documentation was reviewed. Several unmarked speculative statements sourced from the late-session AI exchanges had entered the report as if verified. The team had to go back through 48 exchanges to identify which outputs to flag.

    C2 · Content

    Four defences for long-session reliability

    Context loss is not a bug that will be fixed. It is the expected behaviour of the underlying architecture. Your job is to manage it.

    The three stages of a session

    Session start
    All instructions, brief, and guardrails are in scope. The model attends to them with full weight.
    Low risk
    Mid-session
    Context filling. Older content approaching the edge of the window. Attention fading but instructions still technically present.
    Medium
    Overflow
    Earliest content (your guardrails and session brief) silently dropped. Model operates with proportionally fewer constraints.
    ⚠️ High
    🌐
    Minority language reality: Non-Latin scripts (Tamil, Telugu, Ge'ez, etc.) tokenise at 2–3 tokens per word — meaning sessions with non-Latin pasted text fill the context window significantly faster than equivalent English content. If you work in a non-Latin script, mid-session risk arrives earlier than you expect.
    D1 Defence 1 · Task Chunking — One task per chat ›

    The principle: Never mix unrelated work in a single long session. A session that covers exegesis, back-translation review, key-term analysis, and formatting a report — over two hours — will be unreliable by the end.

    Starting a new chat costs you nothing. A fresh chat starts with a full, clean context window ready for your brief.

    ⚠️
    One critical step: A new chat is blank. The AI has no memory of previous sessions by default — it does not know your project, your guardrails, or what was decided last week. Before typing your first prompt in any new session, paste your AI Session Brief. Skip this step and you are briefing a stranger who has never heard of your project.
    D2 Defence 2 · Session Refresh — Reinject constraints mid-session ›

    The principle: In long sessions, repeat key constraints at natural pause points — when starting a new passage, a new task type, or after a significant digression.

    A single reinforcing line is often enough:

    Reminder: do not speculate beyond the text. Flag all uncertain interpretations as NEEDS VERIFICATION. Do not suggest renderings for terms not in our key-terms file.

    This is not a sign of bad prompting. It is the correct response to a known architectural behaviour.

    D3 Defence 3 · The AI Session Brief ›

    The principle: Write your standing instructions once, save them as a text file, and paste them at the start of every new chat. This is the most impactful single habit change most practitioners can make.

    The brief replaces ad-hoc re-briefing and ensures guardrails are reset at the start of every task. Structure:

    ## AI Session Brief — [Project Name] ## Project context - Project: [language name] NT / [book / stage] - Team: [team composition] - Stage: [e.g. Checking Stage 2 / FIA] ## Target audience - [Language community, oral / literate] - [Cultural frame — e.g. shame-honour] - [Reading level of primary audience] ## Standing guardrails - Do not speculate beyond the text - Flag all uncertain interpretations as NEEDS VERIFICATION - Do not cite specific reference codes — I will verify independently - Do not suggest renderings not in our key-terms file ## Style rules - Reading level: [primary / secondary / expert] - Register: [formal / conversational / plain] - Avoid: [specific vocabulary or framing] ## Output format defaults - Tables use: Issue | Verse | Suggestion - Flag unchecked items as: NEEDS VERIFICATION

    The brief costs three minutes to write once. It saves you re-briefing at the start of every session and prevents the silent erosion of constraints that happens when you rely on memory alone.

    D4 Defence 4 · System Prompts — Permanent guardrails ›

    The principle: In tools that support it, move your standing instructions into a system prompt — a special instruction layer that sits outside the conversation scroll and is always visible, regardless of session length.

    ToolHow to set a system prompt
    Claude ProjectsCreate a new Project; add instructions in the Project Instructions field. All chats within the Project inherit those instructions.
    Custom GPTs (OpenAI)In the GPT editor, add instructions in the System field. All chats with that GPT use those instructions.
    API accessThe system parameter in the request.
    🏗️
    Lane 3 note: This is the architectural upgrade from individual best-practice to team-level reliability. A project-specific Claude Project or Custom GPT — with the AI Session Brief, key-term list, and theological guardrails as the system prompt — means every team member starts every session with fully correct constraints.

    Choosing the right tool: data sensitivity

    🔴
    One question determines which tool to use: "Is this draft text published?"

    Yes → cloud tools are acceptable.
    No → use a local model (LM Studio) or an enterprise-licensed API only.

    Never paste unpublished draft text into a free consumer chat interface. Free tiers of ChatGPT, Gemini, and similar tools may use session data for model training. Treat them like a public forum: do not share anything you would not publish openly.
    ℹ️
    Your organisation may have stricter policies than this baseline. If your IT or administration team has an approved tool list or data handling policy, follow it. Treat this decision rule as the floor, not the ceiling.
