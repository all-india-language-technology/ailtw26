# Using Prompts to Understand Scripture (Learning that LASTS Design)

## Context
**WHO:** Mixed group from the AI Tools Track — translators, translation consultants, consultants-in-training (CITs), translation advisors, language technology staff, and project facilitators. Facilitator: Ben. Participants work individually or in pairs on their own devices.
**WHY (Core Goal):** Participants have already seen what effective prompting looks like. This session gives them structured, hands-on practice applying those skills specifically to Scripture — moving from understanding the technique to developing the habit of reaching for it when they sit with a text.
**WHEN:** 45 minutes. Day 3, first session of the day — participants arrive fresh, with prompt engineering concepts from the previous day still active in their minds.
**WHERE:** Workshop room — projector, participant laptops or phones with internet access, live AI tool (ChatGPT, Copilot, or similar). Scenarios displayed on screen and shared digitally.
**HOW MUCH (Resources):** Projector, participant laptops or devices, live AI tool access. No printed handouts required. Two participant-sourced scenarios used in C3.

## The 3 WHATs (Evidence of Change)
- **Evidence of Learning (Objective):** By the end of the session, participants will have worked through two scripture scenarios using structured prompts — the first guided as a group with Ben, the second attempted independently in pairs — demonstrating that they can move beyond surface-level questions to prompts that surface deeper exegetical or translation insights.
- **Evidence of Transfer (Outcome):** When participants return to their work and face a difficult passage — an unclear referent, a cultural gap, a tricky key term, an exegetical question — they will reach for a structured prompt rather than guessing or spending hours in resources alone. They will also know how to upload a relevant document (commentary excerpt, lexicon entry, back-translation, project notes) or paste a link to elicit responses grounded in that specific source, treating AI as a contextualized dialogue partner rather than a generic search engine.
- **Evidence of Impact:** Translation teams develop a shared practice of using AI-assisted scripture exploration as a normal part of the exegetical workflow — reducing bottlenecks when consultants are unavailable, and enabling mother-tongue translators and linguists to engage more deeply with difficult passages on their own.

## The HOW: The 5 C's (Module Guide)

### 1. Connection (Hook)
- **Time:** 00:00–00:05 (5 min)
- **Who:** Ben facilitates; whole room participates
- **The Task / Problem:**
    - *Display:* Ben puts a real scenario submitted by a participant on screen — without commentary:
        > *"Our 3-member NE tribal translation team wants to do an exegetical check of an NT book we drafted. We use an SF-generated draft with GNTD as the source, and also refer to NIV, TNN, and other resources. We are MTTs with Bible college training and 6–8 years of experience — but no formal training in meaning-based translation principles. How do we do a systematic exegetical check?"*
    - *Task:* Everyone writes a prompt they would use for that scenario — 90 seconds, silent, no discussion.
    - *Collect:* Ben takes 3–4 responses (typed into chat, shown on screen, or called out).
    - *Compare:* The room looks at the prompts side by side: "Which prompt goes deeper into the text? What makes the difference?"
    - *Hook:* The contrast between shallow and structured prompts creates the felt need that drives the rest of the session. No answer is given yet.
- **ASK mapping:** Attitude/Feelings (Heart) — Disrupting complacency; participants feel the gap between what they wrote and what a better prompt can do. Skill/Actions (Hands) — First active attempt before any instruction.

### 2. Content (Examine Resource)
- **Time:** 00:05–00:15 (10 min)
- **Who:** Ben presents; participants follow along on their own devices
- **The Task / Problem:**
    - *Quick recap (3 min):* Ben displays the anatomy of a good prompt on screen — role, context, task, constraints — as a reference scaffold. This is a reminder, not a re-teach.
    - *Live demo 1 — File upload (4 min):* Ben opens the AI tool, uploads a document (e.g. a commentary excerpt or a lexicon entry), and asks a question grounded in that specific content. He shows how the response changes when the AI is working from a given source rather than its general knowledge.
    - *Live demo 2 — Pasting a link (3 min):* Ben pastes a URL as a reference source and demonstrates eliciting contextualized responses from it. He notes where tools differ in how they handle links vs. uploaded documents.
    - *Invite:* "Note the pattern — you'll use it in the next round."
- **ASK mapping:** Knowledge/Ideas (Head) — Two concrete input methods added to their prompting repertoire. Skill/Actions (Hands) — Follow-along on own devices begins the muscle memory.

### 3. Challenge (Wrestle with Problem)
- **Time:** 00:15–00:35 (20 min)
- **Who:** Round 1 — Ben models, whole group watches/responds; Round 2 — participants work in pairs, Ben circulates

#### Round 1 — Guided (00:15–00:25, 10 min)
- **Scenario:** A 3-member NE tribal translation team (MTTs, Bible college graduates, 6–8 years experience, Tibeto-Burman language family, no formal training in meaning-based translation principles) is doing an exegetical check of an NT book drafted by one team member. They are using an SF-generated draft with GNTD as the source, and referring also to NIV, TNN, and other resources. The check may be part of or follow a team-check focused on naturalness.
- *Task:* Ben thinks aloud and builds a prompt sequence live — modelling how to help the team surface exegetical questions they lack formal training to catch systematically. He demonstrates: (1) setting role and context; (2) asking for interpretive options on a disputed verse; (3) uploading a reference document to ground the response.
- *Debrief:* "What did you notice about how the prompt was built? What would you have done differently?"
- **ASK mapping:** Skill/Actions (Hands) — Watching a complete prompt-building sequence. Knowledge/Ideas (Head) — Seeing how role, context, and document upload combine in a real exegetical task.

#### Round 2 — Pairs (00:25–00:35, 10 min)
- **Scenario (submitted by a participant):** In Paratext's RSV+ Enhanced resources, only the word dictionary is linked. Participants wish there were an Enhanced Resource where descriptions of Jewish cultural phrases and idioms are available at a click — material useful for understanding the text and for writing footnotes or glossary entries. That resource doesn't exist yet. Their task: build a prompt (with a document upload or a link) that gives them what they need for a passage they're working on.
- *Task:* Pairs craft and run their own prompts. Ben circulates, asks questions, nudges without giving answers.
- *Debrief:* One or two pairs share their prompt and output. Room reacts: "Did that go deep enough? What would you change?"
- **ASK mapping:** Skill/Actions (Hands) — Independent prompt construction for a real-type translation need. Attitude/Feelings (Heart) — "I can build this myself."

### 4. Change (Apply)
- **Time:** 00:35–00:42 (7 min)
- **Who:** Individual or pair; Ben circulates, then facilitates brief share-out
- **The Task / Problem:**
    - *Task:* Each participant (or pair) picks one passage or question from their own current translation project and writes a prompt for it — right now, in the room.
    - *Share out:* One or two participants share what they wrote and what response they got. The room reacts briefly: "Would you use that?" "What would make it stronger?"
    - *Goal:* Every participant leaves the room with something genuinely useful — a prompt result relevant to their own work.
- **ASK mapping:** Skill/Actions (Hands) — Applying the pattern to their own real-world context. Attitude/Feelings (Heart) — Ownership; "This works for my project."

### 5. Closure (Summarize)
- **Time:** 00:42–00:45 (3 min)
- **Who:** Ben facilitates; participants speak
- **The Task / Problem:**
    - *Question to the room:* "What is one thing you will do differently next time you sit with a difficult passage?"
    - *Collect:* Ben takes 3–4 quick responses — no elaboration required. Participants speak, not Ben.
    - *Close:* Ben ties back to the opening: "At the start of this session, you wrote a prompt. You now write the second kind." No lecture. One sentence and done.
- **ASK mapping:** Attitude/Feelings (Heart) — Leaving with a clear, self-stated commitment. Knowledge/Ideas (Head) — Consolidating the arc from Connection to Change in a single recalled contrast.
